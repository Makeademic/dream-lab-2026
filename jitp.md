Crafting Creativity in Digital Humanities: The Joys of Empowering Makers to Transform Communities
===

One of the core pedagogical aims of my scholarly persona, the “makeademic,” is to empower people to reenvision themselves as makers. A particularly valuable aspect of the maker identity is that it is much less daunting than asking someone to call themselves an artist, designer, craftsperson, or artisan. Those identities feel reserved for professionals, and there is an emphasis on expertly creating a final polished product (the artwork, the design, the craft, the handiwork) that has no direct equivalence for the maker (perhaps closest is the artifact?). The democratization of the maker subculture is rooted in the well-established DIY ethos from the 70s punk scene while expanding at the beginning of the 21st century with the increased availability of emerging technologies. Two key factors to the maker movement’s success were 1) the establishment of makerspaces as sites for collaborating and sharing resources, and 2) the widespread support of open-source principles that reduced barriers to entry. These core tenets coincide with Lisa Spiro’s proposed values for digital humanities (an interdisciplinary academic umbrella term for humanistic inquiry that thoughtfully engages with/and incorporates technology): 1) openness, 2) collaboration, 3) collegiality and connectedness, 4) diversity, and 5) experimentation (Spiro 2012). 

David Staley posed the question: “Now that the maker movement has come to the humanities, and digital fabrication tools and computer numerical control (CNC) machines are potential parts of the humanist’s tool kit, what sorts of things will humanists make?” (Staley 2017, 32). At the intersection of making and digital humanities is critical making, “a mode of materially productive engagement that is intended to bridge the gap between creative physical and conceptual exploration” (Ratto 2011, 252). So how does research through making show up in DH scholarship, classroom, communities, and how does making highlight the relationship we have with our communities? This article will first review collaborative critical making DH scholarship, then explore how DH educators are incorporating “learning by making” in their classrooms and beyond, and finally highlight how making can build and transform communities.

# Educating and Empowering Makers

The collaborative projects from the previous section showcase how material making can enhance humanistic inquiry, but how do those skills get built in the first place? While some DH scholars may have backgrounds in engineering, design, and fine arts, we cannot assume that every humanist can jump into a maker project. Therefore, it is necessary for many educators in traditional classrooms as well as extracurricular programming to teach these skills in an accessible manner. In this section, I will feature some of the learning by making approaches that have inspired my own teaching, as well as share from the workshops I have taught as an alt-ac DH professional.

## Undergraduate Classrooms
In the article “Bibliocircuitry and the Design of the Alien Everyday” collaboratively authored by professor Kari Kraus and several of her students from her seminar, “Book 2.0: The History of the Book and the Future of Reading,” the authors introduce their concept “bibliocircuitry” as “thinking of physical books as platforms for experimenting with computation“ (Hancock 2013, 78). Based on the range of projects presented, the students had a lot of freedom to explore their creativity with different technologies. Clifford Hichar, for example, combines more analog craft skills like altered books and erasure poetry with the Lilypad Arduino microcontroller to animate lights behind illustrations. Cameron Hozafari also takes advantage of how traditional craft supplies like graphite pencil can be used to complete circuits, connecting a printed book to a Makey Makey microcontroller to play audio.

Ten years later, professors Maggie Melo and Laura March detail their course project “Bibliocircuitry: Old Books, New Ideas” for their *Information Professionals in the Makerspace: Critical Theories, Applications, and Practices* course. This example is an interesting inverse, where the course topic was makerspaces and the book is used as a vehicle to explore the concept. One of the requirements of the project was that “the artifact included the thoughtful and intentional integration of at least four of the following technologies: 3D printing, lasercutting, augmented reality, paper automata, circuits, and microcontrollers” (Melo and March 2023, 146). One student created a pop-up phoenix book combining acrylic paint with a paper circuit, imitating the fire of the mythical creature. Another student used a book as a platform to create a stage for a 3D-printed ballerina with a laser-cut background scene and LEDs to mimic stage lights. The authors discuss two aspects of joy, both the joy associated with pride/satisfaction in problem-solving/actualizing their concept as well as the joy of having others interact with their books.

## Library Workshops
Located in the Kislak Center for Special Collections, Rare Books and Manuscripts, the [Vitale Lab for Book History and Digital Humanities](https://www.library.upenn.edu/spaces/bhdhlab) is a book lab with a text technologies petting zoo, a Book Beetle desktop printing press, microscope and document cameras, zines and ephemera hung with colorful clothespins, and a small reference library. This space is directed by Dot Porter, Curator of Digital Humanities in the Schoenberg Institute for Manuscript Studies, and Whitney Trettien, Faculty Director of the Price Lab for Digital Humanities. The lab is uniquely situated in the library with access to rare book materials for visualization but also serves as a makerspace with tools and materials for making prints, zines, buttons, and other paper crafts. While we call this space a lab, it might alternatively be identified as a feminist hackerspace; “This merging of hackerspace activities with traditionally feminized forms of craft-based practices destabilizes dominant categories of technical work to recognize the technical practices that are already taking place” (Burek et al 2017, 226-227). 

Dot Porter invited me and Christine Kemp, Program Coordinator of Technology and Play, to collaborate with her Reimagining Illumination workshop series, where participants learned how to create illuminated initials with gold leaf, paint 3D-printed replicas of manuscript marginalia characters, cut and paste replica manuscript collages, etc. As a longtime fan of [high-low tech](https://highlowtech.org/) and former design team member of [Chibitronics](https://chibitronics.com/), I wanted to design a workshop introducing people to the magic of combining paper with electronics. I designed a template to create mini light-up constellation shadow boxes. This activity teaches the fundamentals of creating a parallel electrical circuit with a coin cell battery, conductive fabric tape, and circuit stickers with built in surface mount LEDs and resistors. The frame was cut and folded from a template printed on holographic cardstock and the historical constellation artwork was laser printed and foil laminated. While this is a simple “craft”, it is an important building block to more advanced projects, such as [interactive installations](https://chibitronics.com/gallery/) or [light-up data visualizations](https://wonderfulidea.co/blog/2018/4/9/mapping-data-visualization-and-paper-circuits).

These workshops required advanced registration but were open to the public and attracted a diverse array of makers including graduate students from education, academic staff from departments across campus, nurses from the university hospital, and retirees from the local Philadelphia area. Several participants enjoyed the first workshop so much, they became regulars in the series, and it created a delightful community where people shared the creative projects that they were working on outside of the workshop, or they came back to tell us how their friends and family members had been impressed by the crafts they brought home. Much of the informal feedback we received specifically mentioned how great it was to take a mental break from their typical workday and revive their creativity. Much like feminist hackerspaces, “the goal is to have ‘a space where you can come into your own but also watch other people learn and grow’” (Burek et al 2017, 225).

## DH Training Institutes
Because digital humanities is such an interdisciplinary field, it is impossible for any one institution to offer comprehensive training. In 2001, the Digital Humanities Summer Institute was founded as a way for digital humanists at all stages of their career to enhance their skills and create networking opportunities for collaboration. The summer institute was hosted at the University of Victoria from 2004 to 2024. Meanwhile, in 2013 the Maryland Institute for Technology in the Humanities started a Digital Humanities Winter Institute, which spun into subsequent years as HILT, the Humanities Intensive Learning and Training Institute, which travelled across host institutions, including 2018 at the University of Pennsylvania. From there and then, the Price Lab for Digital Humanities at the University of Pennsylvania started offering Dream Lab as a low-cost East Coast digital humanities summer training alternative for graduate students and early career DH professionals.

From 2013-2016, members of the Maker Lab @UVic taught the [Physical Computing and Desktop Fabrication](https://github.com/uvicmakerlab/dhsi2016/blob/master/index.md) course, which I had the privilege of taking as a graduate student in 2016. Ten years later, it was exciting to design my own DH summer training course for Dream Lab. Inspired by the recent trends in the #DHMakes community and Kari Kraus’ “The Care of Enchanted Things” in *Debates in the Digital Humanities 2019*, I titled my course [Critical Making: Enchanted Things](https://makeademic.github.io/dream-lab-2026/).

> “Enchanted things refers to hybrid physical-digital objects in the real world whose seemingly magical properties imbue them with the aura of enchantment” (Kraus 2019).
> This hands-on course offers an introduction to critical making (thinking through prototyping) in digital humanities. The goal of this course is to gain practical takeaways that can be used in research and pedagogy. We will explore a variety of analog and digital technologies including but not limited to letterpress and bookmaking, 3D scanning and printing, vinyl and laser cutters, physical computing and paper circuits. At the end of the week, participants will use the technologies available to create enchanted things.

In the true collaborative spirit of digital humanities, this course was co-created and co-taught by my amazing colleagues across campus. I approached all of them with general ideas of how we might learn from their spaces, and each offered their time, resources, and expertise to help create a better project that participants would enjoy. They each welcomed us into the space, contextualizing their work, and let us engage in playful experimentation, and I am eternally grateful.

### Unit 1: Paper Circuits
In our first unit, I introduced participants to the Vitale Lab for Book History and Digital Humanities and presented on enchanted things, #DHMakes, and data physicalization. We then started our paper circuit activity, a modified version of the [Chibitronics Silhouettes Lesson](https://chibitronics.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/Chibitronics-Educators-Guide-1.pdf) (p. 45). Participants drew a design on the front of their card, then traced their circuit with conductive fabric tape on the inside to connect the negative side of the coin cell battery to the negative leg (anode) of the LED and the positive side of the battery to the positive leg (cathode) of the LED with a break for a switch. Participants then wrote a message using Sharpie on a piece of vellum paper and used double-sided tape to attach it to the back side of the front of the card. As an enchanted thing, when the switch was pressed, the light would turn on, revealing the hidden message strategically placed in the design of the card.

![Photo of paper circuit card with yellow box and blue background and a yellow light that reveals the word BELIEVE](https://github.com/Makeademic/dream-lab-2026/blob/70e5a49418d59dc1e0162748e72b562978c55a2e/BelievePaperCircuit.jpg)

### Unit 2: Laser Cutters
In our second unit, Taylor Caputo, Director of the [Engineering Studios @ Venture Lab](https://venturelab.upenn.edu/engineering-studios) gave us a safety tour of the space, which included 3D printers, laser-cutters, rotary saws, a spray booth, and a fume hood. She then presented on ways that the space has been utilized, including her popular [How To Make Things course](https://penntoday.upenn.edu/news/sewing-3d-printing-taylor-caputo-and-engineering-studios-penns-venture-lab), and taught us about how to prepare files for laser cutters to either vector or raster etch or vector cut and how to optimize settings for speed and desired look. She then started our laser-cutting project by directing us to create an illustration on a template. The studio technicians then took a photo to convert to a digital file, and within a few hours, we had our illustrations raster etched and cut out of clear acrylic. As an enchanted thing, when the acrylic is placed on an LED base, it creates an edge-lit acrylic lamp where the color of the light appears only at the etched design.

![Photo of edge-lit acrylic lamp that reads enchanted things](https://github.com/Makeademic/dream-lab-2026/blob/70e5a49418d59dc1e0162748e72b562978c55a2e/EnchantedThingsLamp.jpg)

### Unit 3: Prototyping with Cardboard
In our third unit, Taylor Caputo introduced us to a wonderful prototyping tool, the [ChompSaw](https://chompshop.com/), which was invented by her former students from her Integrated Product Development class (and was developed as a very successful [Kickstarter](https://www.kickstarter.com/projects/chompshop/inventable-a-kid-safe-power-tool-for-cutting-cardboard) project). It is a safety power tool for children and adults alike to create low-fidelity prototypes with cardboard. Taylor prepared pieces of cardboard with printed templates glued to them, and from there we used the ChompSaws to cut out pieces of the pattern and use hot glue to attach them. As an enchanted thing, when we assembled the pieces, we had cardboard puppets that we brought to life with accessories.

![Photo of green cardboard puppet with a pipecleaner handlebar mustache](https://github.com/Makeademic/dream-lab-2026/blob/fe654c6e9556bd564dba32c7cd99c0c3f9535400/GreenPuppet.jpg)

### Unit 4: Physical Computing with Arduino
In our fourth unit, I introduced participants to the open-source community Arduino with the [Grove Beginner Kit for Arduino](https://www.digikey.com/en/products/detail/seeed-technology-co-ltd/110061162/12093286). This kit contains a Seeeduino Lotus microcontroller with breakaway sensors and actuators and is accompanied by extensive online [documentation](https://wiki.seeedstudio.com/Grove-Beginner-Kit-For-Arduino/). We followed the first five lessons to get used to reading and writing code, verifying and uploading code to the board, and understanding sensors (input) and actuators (output). As an enchanted thing, we created an eerie pseudo-theremin concert by everyone adjusting the values in their [Arduino code](https://app.arduino.cc/sketches/29661918-eca8-4f81-a2a7-b5fe93844d4e?view-mode=embed ) to play a different range of Hertz values on their piezo buzzer based on the amount of input coming in from the photocell (light sensor). 

### Unit 5: 3D Scanning Bookbindings
In our fifth unit, Dot Porter showcased two recent acquisitions, [Sentiments and Similes of William Shakespeare](https://find.library.upenn.edu/catalog/9979614004403681) and [Parables of Our Lord](https://find.library.upenn.edu/catalog/9979468820103681) by historic maker, Henry Noels Humphreys, which both have exquisite chromolithographic illuminations inside and ornate papier-mâché bindings on the outside. Kathryn Reuter, Fine Arts Librarian, then introduced us to best methods for 3D scanning with the Artec Spider and participants had the opportunity to practice the art of capturing volumetric data. As an enchanted thing, we took inspiration of this genre of gift books, and applied it to our next unit on 3D printing.

### Unit 6: 3D Printing and NFCs
In our sixth unit, Tex Kang and Christine Kemp, Program Coordinators for Technology and Play at Education Commons introduced us to their makerspace filled with 3D printers, CNC vinyl cutters, physical computing components, an embroidery machine, some metalsmithing tools. In advance of the workshop, Tex Kang 3D-printed each participant a [living hinge tome keychain](https://thangs.com/designer/Jaatinen3D/3d-model/Tome%20Keychain%20Stash%20Container-1116864) that can open and snap close. He explained how he was able to pause the print partway to switch from the colored filament to the white filament. The first component to the enchanted thing was using a holographic build plate to mimic the mother-of-pearl sheen found on many papier-mâché gift books. In the second component to the enchanted thing, Tex Kang taught participants how to program small near-field communication (NFC) stickers to direct a mobile device to a URL using the [NFC Tools](https://nfc.software/) mobile app. We then embedded the NFCs in our book keychains and they served as quick and portable links to academic webpages.

![Photo of pink book keychain with white pages and a silver NFC sticker inside](https://github.com/Makeademic/dream-lab-2026/blob/fe654c6e9556bd564dba32c7cd99c0c3f9535400/NFCBook.jpg)

### Unit 7: Anaglyph Letterpress
In our seventh unit, Jessica Peterson, Common Press Manager introduced us to her letterpress studio, which has a 19th century handpress, a platen press, and two Vandercook cylinder presses, as well as many drawers of metal and wood type. Jessica Peterson presented on the history of typography and letterpress as well as her collaborations with historians on re-creating the printed Declaration of Independence. This activity was inspired by the anaglyph letterpress workshop offered by Kadin Henningsen, Head of the King Library Press at the University of Kentucky, and he generously shared his experimentation to find the perfect Pantone color ([319](https://www.pantone.com/color-finder/319-C)) for the cyan. Jessica Peterson mixed the ink, and each participant learned how to typeset their own word out of wood type and create a lockup. We then used brayers to directly apply the cyan ink to the type, register and print the text on cover paper, clean off the ink, offset the paper, and reprint the word again in red ink. As an enchanted thing, the resulting red-cyan offset word when viewed with 3D glasses appears to float in front of the paper. 

![This anaglyph letterpress print reads Past (shifted back), Present (picture plane), and Future (shifted forward) when viewed with red/cyan 3D glasses ](https://github.com/Makeademic/dream-lab-2026/blob/42589360d9d26533ffd2deeb90681cbedd2cd2ff/PastPresentFutureAnaglyph.jpg)

### Unit 8: Vector Design, Vinyl Cutting, and Screenprinting
In our eighth unit, I introduced participants to an iterative process for creating screenprints from digital designs. First, participants had to get comfortable with designing vector graphics on their computers. Then I led them through the process of mirroring their designs and cutting them out of vinyl with the Silhouette Cameo hobby CNC cutter. Participants then painstakingly removed the positive elements of their design, leaving behind the negative to transfer to silkscreen using transfer tape. Once their screens were prepared, participants helped each other use squeegees to pull screenprinting ink across their screens with cardstock or fabric below. As an enchanted thing, participants had the option to use a phantom clear ink which dries clear and hydrophopic, so the message is only revealed when the design is exposed to water.

![Photo of blue towel with white text that reads The Most Massively Useful Thing, and hydrophobic message reads wet for use in hand to hand combat](https://github.com/Makeademic/dream-lab-2026/blob/70e5a49418d59dc1e0162748e72b562978c55a2e/PhantomClearTowel.jpg)

### Reflections/ Show-and-Tell
The best part about Dream Lab is the community created. I am so grateful I had the opportunity to collaborate with so many brilliant people including independent scholars, graduate students, academic staff, librarians, and faculty. In advance of the course, I sent out an email with a packing list, a to-do list, recommended reading, and the course mindset: “Mistakes are portals of discovery. Take chances, make mistakes, get messy!” Much like the #DHMakes community’s #DHTries and JITP’s [Teaching Fails](https://cuny.manifoldapp.org/projects/journal-of-interactive-technology-and-pedagogy-no-teaching-fails), I think it’s important to recognize how much more learning happens when things are not perfect. I am particularly grateful that every single participant approached the course with curiosity, creativity, patience, and overall good vibes. When the screenprinting activity ended up taking too long and getting pushed from Thursday to Friday, participants were open-minded, and though they only had an hour and a half solely dedicated to individual projects on Friday, they ended up creating incredibly impressive projects through tinkering and a can-do spirit. For example, one participant combined two inverted edge-lit acrylic lamps to create a multi-color light sculpture based on historical scientific illustrations. Another participant created an interactive art installation of everyday objects with hidden NFC tags. Another participant created a bibliocircuitry project based on a historic cookbook from their collection, programming text to scroll across an OLED screen and an LED to flicker. As I’m sure many educators can relate, there is an incredible joy and pride in seeing something new created as a direct outcome of teaching. My hope is that by sampling beginner projects with a range of technologies, everyone was able to take away at least one maker activity that they could bring back to their own research and pedagogy and know how to pursue additional resources to become more advanced. Furthermore, as I stated at the beginning, my aim is to empower makers. The authors of *Maker-centered learning* define maker empowerment as a “sensitivity to the designed dimension of objects and systems, along with the inclination and capacity to *shape one's world* through building, tinkering, re/designing, or hacking” (Clapp et al. 2016, italics added). The idea of shaping one’s world I think is particularly meaningful as it relates to the intersection of maker empowerment and social justice. In the final section, I will discuss how research in making helps build and transform community.

# Building and Transforming Community

While I (and many others) mourn the loss of the heyday of academic Twitter, I have been delighted to follow along and share with the #DHMakes hashtag, a vibrant, generous community of makers currently on Bluesky. It started as a request from Bethany Nowviskie to share something creative that brings joy as a “timeline cleanse,” and thanks to Quinn Daedal, Jacque Wernimont, and Kim Martin, became an organized hashtag of creative maker projects like embroidery samplers, noodle knitting, brioche, woolen-spun yarn, etc. “None of these examples had a direct connection to digital humanities scholarship, but the act of sharing something meaningful, joyful, and beautiful struck a chord with several DH scholars – particularly women and non-binary people” (Visconti et al. 2024). I know I enjoy participating in the community to share and learn about resources, brainstorm ideas, cheer each other on in progress, and most importantly spread joy. Poet Toi Derricotte gifted us the line “Joy is an act of resistance” which has been recirculated in so many protest signs and other visual media and I often return to this message when evaluating why I’m making something.

Amanda Visconti, Director of the Scholar’s Lab at the University of Virginia, has frequently shared their empowering letterpress poster designs with the #DHMakes community. Some posters incorporate [puns and other wordplay](https://wolfproofpress.com/assets/images/highlights/Visconti-Print-BookmakersSay-ChipRedGradient.jpg), others have [colorful gradients](https://wolfproofpress.com/assets/images/highlights/Visconti-Print-Series-TransFuturesFriendsSelf.png), still others engage in [tactical frivolity](https://wolfproofpress.com/assets/images/highlights/Visconti-Print-Series-TheRevolutionWontBeTelevisedRemix.png). Not only do they have prints and stickers that benefit mutual aid but they also distribute free advocacy posters. Other letterpress makers that have worked towards transforming their communities include Dylan Lewis “rage-printing” posters with the trans flag background with text that reads “protect TRANS kids” and [Nat McGartland](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=2G9GZjRdd44) who was featured in the [NY Times](https://www.nytimes.com/2022/05/03/us/politics/supreme-court-protests-abortion.html) for bringing the BookBeetle letterpress to create posters reading “my BODY, my CHOICE” for the protest of Supreme Court overturning Roe v. Wade. These crafts may not be “digital” humanities, but they are very much “technological” humanities, albeit historical analog technologies, and very much “public” humanities.

As an educator, I have incorporated maker assignments with an aim of reaching a larger community. In the [Advanced Exposition: Codexposition](https://codexposition.tumblr.com/) course I taught in 2018, we took inspiration from the Tactile Picture Books Project, and students used 3D printing to create tactile picture books for children who have low vision and then wrote a how-to guide for the Instructables community. One student created a [guide](https://www.instructables.com/3D-Printed-Childrens-Tactile-Book/) that has been viewed over 4000 times. At the end of the course, the student [reflected](https://codexposition.tumblr.com/post/180814267849/final-reflections), “the idea of having work I created out in the world instead of locked in some ‘past enrollments’ folder on Canvas is empowering... I feel much more confident now further exploring the resources available to me here [on campus]... Part of [the class] that I didn’t expect was how it helped me to connect more to my friends and family.... I hope that connection continues after this class ends.” These reflections demonstrate that learning by making led to a stronger sense of agency and helped the student connect to an online community as well as reconnect with their individual community. 

In an effort to connect with my new local maker community in Philadelphia, I collaborated with Hannah Tardie, Makerspace Manager of the Loretta C. Duckworth Scholars Studio, to propose a Screenprinting for Community Repair workshop for the Electronics Faire at Temple University. They provided generous and generative feedback that helped me shape the two-day workshop into a success. I was inspired by the reparative community work being done by [Art Price Studios](https://www.youtube.com/shorts/xfmUDYKALh0), a cooperative screenprinting space that became a community hub and resource for folks who wanted to print messages of resistance on their own clothes in Minneapolis. In line with the theme of the Faire, the idea of the workshop was to both “repair” by upcycling old clothes and also “repair” community morale, strengthening connections of crafting and activism. The Scholars Studio already had an industry vinyl cutter, vinyl, and transfer tape, so the only supplies we had to order were screens, squeegees, and ink. 

During the workshop, I introduced the design thinking framework:
1. Frame a Question: How might we create a design for our community so that we repair what’s broken?
2. Gather Inspiration: Research protest signs, shirts, quote posters, memes, icons
3. Generate ideas: sticky notes, perhaps consider pop culture references, short phrases
4. Make ideas tangible: Make quick sketches
5. Refine: Move from sketch to computer
6. Share: Cut vinyl, screenprint for community
   
And the design constraints:

1. Time: What can we accomplish in this short workshop?
2. Technical Expertise: How well do we know design software?
3. Artistic Ability: Can we express our message with just words?
4. Material/Color: What can we do with black, white, and gold ink?

I shared slides on wordplay and design in protest that incorporated puns, alliteration, rhymes, etc. as well as an introduction to typography and some resources on vector design. We then watched Jennifer Sanderson’s tutorial on screenprinting with craft vinyl together. The participants were a range of local artists and makers, community activists, graduate students, and academic staff, and they created incredible protest signs. My favorite part was that after I taught the first person how to pull their print (you have to apply some pressure to the squeegee while dragging it at an angle), they each taught the next person how to do it while I was busy helping with the digital designs and vinyl cutting.

While I specifically framed the Electronics Faire workshop with the theme of Community Repair, I left the maker activities of the Critical Making: Enchanted Things course more open to personal preference, so I was delighted on the last day of Dream Lab to discover that one participant had hidden a powerful message on their first day with the paper circuit activity. The participant created a pencil illustration of the Statue of Liberty’s torch, and when you press the switch, a green light glows, illuminating the message “Abolish ICE.” Rather than operating on a binary of either serious or joyful, the participant saw the potential to simultaneously experience the joys of making while also advocating for community transformation. This inspired me, and I look forward to sharing this work as a model in future iterations of this paper circuit workshop that I have planned for high school students in Philadelphia next month.

As I stated from the beginning, my aim is to empower people to become makers. If you are interested in demystifying technology, flexing your creative muscles, building community, and transforming the world around you, consider this your formal invitation to the Good Necessary Trouble Makers Club; we’re making our own T-shirts.
